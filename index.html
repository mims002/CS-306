Background / Scenario

Background: Linus is a student at Dale University. His major at the university is computer science and he is currently in his third year at the school. One of the courses he is currently taking is CS 367 this semester and strives to be the best student possible. Dale University is known for having an excellent computer science department. All of the computer science courses there, including CS 367, are known to be designed to be difficult to ensure that the students that graduate are the best they can be. In addition to this, the university takes the academic integrity of its students very seriously and makes a great effort to prevent any form of academic dishonesty among students.

Scenario: Linus is a struggling student in professor Mauler’s CS 367 class. A couple days before one of professor Mauler’s exams a flag appeared warning the IT department that someone had been browsing through professor Mauler’s Hercules directories. Jack Smith, a member of the Dale university’s IT department, was working at the time the flag appeared, he immediately notified the university about the suspicious activity and professor Mauler. The following day the university discovered whose account had been browsing through professor Mauler’s Hercules directories, Linus. The university took swift action, instead of trying to solve the issue inhouse they decided to get the police involved and brought up charges against Linus.

Legal Elements

For this case there are two main legal questions raised. The first of these is whether Linus hacked into the Hercules server. This question deals with a potential violation of the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act ( 18 U.S.C. § 1030),which concerns the improper access of a computer without proper authority and having the intent to cause harm through that access. The Computer Fraud and Abuse Act has several factors that the defendant must be proven to have committed to show a violation on the part of the act. These factors are:

  1. Knowingly accessing a computer without authorization or exceeding authorized access
  2. Intentionally accessing a computer without authorization or exceeding authorized access
  3. Knowingly and with intent to defraud, accessing a protected computer without authorization
  4. Hacking into or illegally using a computer to change information, such as grades, work reports, etc (18 U.S.C § 1030(g))
  5. Using someone else’s computer to access personal information with the intent to use such fraudulently (18 U.S.C § 1030(g))
  6. The term "exceeds authorized access” means to access a computer with authorization and to use such access to obtain or alter information in the computer that the accessor is not entitled so to obtain or alter (18 U.S.C § 1030(e)(6))

The second legal question concerns whether the university has a right to search Linus’ computer. This creates a seventh legal element in the case dealing with search and seizure. The 4th Amendment of the Constitution says that the search and seizure of person’s personal effects can’t be done without a warrant and probable cause. This will require the plaintiff to argue that they have probable cause and should be issued a warrant to search the laptop of the defendant.

The defendant, Linus Williams, must prove that he was not able to login to the Hercules server at the time of the flag because he was out with his friend Han Khan at the gym and to eat dinner. Han Khan’s witness statement can prove Linus’s alibi.

The defendant side can also bring up the fact that just because Linus’s account was flagged perusing through his professor’s directories doesn’t automatically mean that he was involved in the criminal activity. There can be many reasons as to why or how someone could have gotten access to Linus’s Hercules account. A similar scenario occurred in a Virginia case, Lester v. Allied Concrete Co. The plaintiff in this case only had a photograph and a suspicion that the defendant had hacked into his Facebook account. The court claimed that the plaintiff cannot make such an unreasonable claim without having solid evidence to backup their claim. So there are many parallels between the Linus’s case and the Lester v. Allied Concrete Co. Linus’s lawyers can use this case as a precedent. They can assert that the plaintiff, the University, needs to provide more evidence to support the claim that Linus was behind the hacking of professor Mauler’s Hercules account. This is another case the lawyers can reference.

A case the defendant side can use as a precedence with great shepardizing record is Commonwealth of Virginia v. Jon Michael Honhart. This case deals with the possible damages the defendant might have cost the plaintiff. In the end the jury ruled in favor of the defendant, because the plaintiff could not supply enough evidence proving the defendant caused any damages to their machines. Linus’s lawyers can use this as precedent and try to prove that the legal elements of the plaintiff’s side are not true. If the defendant side can plant a seed of doubt in the eyes of the jury or judge, they have a chance of the jury or judge ruling in their favor.

The Dale University v. Williams case is well balanced because both sides have evidence that can potentially tip the balance of the case in their favor. The plaintiff has clear evidence of someone using Linus’s account to hack into the Hercules server due to the flag and the tampering of student grades. On the other hand, the defendant has a strong alibi and potential evidence to show there could be issues with the server that could have made it seem like Linus hacked into the server. The balance of the case is also dependent on the defendants revealing the plaintiff’s secrets, mainly about the Professor Mauler having Alzheimer. The Alzheimer factor can counter Mauler’s statement that some of the grades have been changed after the time of the alleged flag, proving that Professor Mauler has Alzheimer could invalidate his statement thereby forcing the court to retract it. The case also depends on the plaintiff revealing private information about the defendant side, mainly about Linus having experience hacking into games and uploading hacking tutorials on Youtube and other platforms. Overall, both sides of this case have an equal opportunity to win.
